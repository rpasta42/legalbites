Jury Awards Over $400K, State Pays Only $100K: The Legal Battle of a Disabled Victim!

Aug 4, 2023
Relief of Reginald Jackson by the City of Lakeland

In a stunning turn of events that pulls at the very fabric of justice and compassion in our society, Senate Bill 20 has set the stage for a legal battle that touches on the rights of the disabled, the accountability of law enforcement, and the limitations imposed by legislative statutes on victim compensation.

At the heart of this legislative drama is Reginald Jackson, whose ordinary life was upended on a brisk October day in 2001 by a mishap that would leave him permanently disabled. A simple typo by Officer Mike Cochran, of the Lakeland Police Department, led to a traffic stop that would spiral into an encounter fraught with negligent actions and irreversible consequences. The Lakeland officer, having misentered a tag number, escalated a benign situation into a horrifying display of force gone wrong.

The subsequent moments were not only chaotic but also tragic. As the details unravel, one can’t help but be gripped by the harrowing account of Mr. Jackson’s plight. A jury, upon reviewing the facts, sided with Jackson, awarding him a life-changing sum of $412,500 – recognition of the severity of his injuries and the profound negligence on part of Officer Cochran. Yet, the rub of the green has been far from kind to Mr. Jackson, as he has been able to collect a mere fraction of that amount – just $100,000 – due to statutory limitations in place at the time of the incident.

Enter Senate Bill 20, a beacon of hope ushered in by Senator Rouson for the 2024 session, with a mission to balance the scales of justice for Mr. Jackson. The bill aims to cut through the red tape that has held back the full compensation deserved to him, proposing that the City of Lakeland be directed to pay the remaining $312,500. As part of the legislation’s terms, this amount would comprehensively cover “all present and future claims related to this incident,” and it explicitly caps attorney fees at 25 percent of the awarded sum.

The facts of this case beckon to the system’s sense of fairness. Court records clearly show that Mr. Jackson was first cited for driving with an unrestrained child – indeed, an offense – yet his subsequent request for assistance to obtain a car seat was met with refusal by Officer Cochran, exacerbating the tension of the encounter. When Mr. Jackson, seeking to use a pay phone on Cochran’s instruction, maneuvered his car, the officer’s response was not just disproportionate – it was devastating. As Mr. Jackson slowly moved the vehicle, Officer Cochran’s bullet pierced through the windshield and struck Mr. Jackson’s neck – a momentary decision with a lifetime of consequences.

The repercussions of that day point to a profound injustice – not only did it leave Mr. Jackson with a disability but also trapped him in a legal limbo where the jury’s award couldn’t translate into full restitution due to an arbitrary cap long enshrined in Florida’s statutes.

Senate Bill 20, therefore, represents not just a legal remedy but a moral imperative, challenging the State to look beyond the cold face of the law and into the eyes of a man whose life has been changed forever by a single, careless act. The question at the center of this debate is whether justice, in its true form, will finally be served.

Is the legislation, with its landmark proposal, poised to correct the scales of justice and offer Mr. Jackson the full measure of compensation that a jury deemed fair? It’s a narrative fraught with tension, as the City of Lakeland’s potential responsibility and the State’s interpretation of its legal statutes hang in the balance.

Now, as SB 20 steps into the spotlight and the measured gavel of legislative resolve looms, we all wait with bated breath. Will Reginald Jackson’s long legal battle culminate in triumph, or will it be stifled by an unwavering adherence to statutory limitations? The answer will soon unfold, setting a precedent for cases like Jackson’s and testing the State’s commitment to rectifying the wrongs inflicted upon those like him, who have suffered at the hands of those sworn to protect and serve.
